Did the retirement of Google Reader leave you in a state of RSS blues? Or even worse, unable to keep up with the interwebs? Fear not, T3 trawled the web for the best 5 RSS reader alternatives.

Gallery below at your disposal.

1. Feedly

Possibly the service most tipped to take over from Google Reader, Feedly has already benefited from its demise, bringing in a host of new features and users to boot. It’s the simple approach that makes it highly recommendable; just log in with your old Google Reader account and all your feeds will return, shown in a modern, slick and picture centric view. Feedly also has companion apps for both iOS and Android so wherever you are you’ll be up-to-date.

Link: Feedly

2. Digg Reader

Digg reader, while not as feature rich as Feedly, is much more visually simple. Its two column view is bare, yet shows all your updates in an easy to read manner. It has efficient keyboard shortcuts for power users and a nifty popular tab to see what other people are reading. If you’re a big fan of Instapaper, Digg could be the reader for you as both are operated by Betaworks so the integration is seamless.

Link: Digg Reader

3. AOL Reader

While still in Beta, AOL Reader is purpose built as a Google Reader replacement. Again, it offers migration from your old accounts and a plethora of sharing options. Its interface is straightforward, with a list of feeds on the left and a picture based view of all your news to the right. As with the other readers, it’s simple and all about delivering and discovering content. Unfortunately app support is lacking and AOL says they have iOS and Android apps on the way, but for now the website displays well enough on mobile devices.

Link: AOL Reader

4. NewsBlur

If you want more features for your buck then NewsBlur could be for you. It focuses on delivering content quickly, updating your feeds every minute so you’re never out of the loop. You can view stories on their original site as well as sharing them with others. While it is free for up to 64 feeds, if you’re a power user it’s going to set you back $24 a year for more. It's not be pretty, but its power features are a definite plus.

Link: NewsBlur
Price: $24 a year (for more than 64 feeds)

5. Pulse

Pulse has been around for a while as a mobile app, but now they’re making a push for the online space. Big pictures are the name of the game, and Pulse puts striking images front and centre. The set up is easy too, enabling you to quickly populate your feeds with news topics such as sport or technology. Google Reader importing is also supported to make a pretty complete package, but probably one for the casual user.

Link: Pulse

It’s the game that’s got gaming wags shakin’ and droolin’. Metro: Last Light takes off from where Metro 2033 left off, going beak, make that bleaker, with Communists, stranded subways and the odd Nazi. T3 Middle East spoke to Huw Beynon, Global Brand Manager for Metro Last Light on the development roadmap and The Art Of Apocalyptic Survival Horror Color Texturing.

Interview: Anil George, Support: Viva Entertainment

a.

What do you think differentiates Metro: Last Light from Metro 2033, and from the larger first-person shooter category games? The amazing graphics machine and far improved physics have been noted by many gamers.

Well, there are two questions here!

Firstly, we feel the Metro series is significantly removed from the vast majority of ‘shooters’ – and this is because our focus has always been on creating a rich, believable world, and providing the player with a deep, meaningful story as a way to explore that world.

We don’t really class Metro as a ‘shooter’ – yes, it has combat elements, but as you have seen, there are large section of the game where you experience very different styles of gameplay, whether that be pure survival, horror, or exploration.

So Metro is first and foremost a ‘1st person story’ that combines gameplay genres and styles that help tell that story.

To the other part of your question – what differentiates Last Light from 2033 – this is a more subtle distinction, as we feel we got the basic  formula right with Metro 2033. We didn’t want to change it fundamentally.

Instead, we focused a lot on refining key ,mechanics – stealth, combat; improving our technology and visual fidelity; exploring a different set of narrative themes; and experimenting with a richer, more colourful palette and art style.

b.

Visually, how did you go about ensuring the sheer bleakness of the environment around the player. While Metro 2033 fell in a relatively straight forward post apocalyptic survival horror category , Last Light seems to be following a more fantasy horror theme….

It’s amazing what a bit of colour can do J. Although we think the Russian setting, and the unique environment of the Metro helped us stand out from that ‘generic’ post-apocalyptic setting, we wanted to try something new for Last Light. I think Metro 2033 mostly avoided the ‘grey brown��� trap that is leveled at a lot of shooters / post-apocalyptic games due to our use of lighting, which gave us a lot of colour themes to add to the underground areas. However, the surface remained this uniformly bleak, desolate, ice-locked landscape full of blues, whites and greys. For Metro: Last Light we knew we didn’t want to just re-tread these environments – as artists, this is actually pretty boring. So we did a few things to change the look of the game, without losing that bleakness. Most important was moving the time frame of the story on a year – in this time, the nuclear winter has begun to thaw, and the first signs of spring are here. Our outdoor environments now have running water, the first signs of vegetation, sunlight breaking through the clouds. We also added day / night cycles and dramatic weather effects – thunder storms, rolling fog and cloud, howling winds blowing debris into your vision – these changes combined to give us a much richer and varied palette when building our outdoor environments, without losing that ‘bleakness’ that defines Metro.

For our interiors, we use many more light sources, and more varied lights – from harsh white strip lights, to soft blue paraffin lamps, to the green glow of luminous fungus – all interplaying to create richer environments. And lastly, we thought a lot about location design – particularly our Station Cities. In D6, Venice and the Bolshoi Theater we created three very distinct environments, from each other and from any of the Stations of Metro 2033.

c.

When the game was originally shown at the 2011 E3 show, it was included in the Wii U show-reel, what were the issues faced in releasing the game on the Wii platform.

It came down to time / resource – or rather a lack of the two. We are a small studio, and we are already trying to release the game on a new platform (PS3 – Metro 2033 was 360 and PC only).When it came to the crunch, we felt that dedicating staff to a Wii U version at the expense  of PC, 360 and PS3 would be to the detriment of the overall quality of the product.

We certainly didn’t mean to mislead anyone at E3 2011 – at that time we were a long way from launch, and it all seemed possible. There is certainly no technical reason why we could not get Metro to run on the platform, given some time and focus to take advantage of the hardware.

d.

A PlayStation 4 version of the game has been announced, what additions can we expect  in the PS4 version of the game?

Ha, it’s not quite announced! We expressed an interest in doing a PS4 version – and yes, we would still like to – but this has yet to be decided. We have no doubt we could easily produce a PS4 version, but it has to make sense commercially. Given we have already successfully released on current gen and PC, is there a big enough market left to justify the time and cost of development? Will people want an ‘old’ game for their ‘new’ PS4, or will they want a new title? Is it good enough to just release a ‘next gen’ version, or do we need to make significant improvements and add more content? These are all things we have to think about, and we do not have the answer yet sorry!

e.

One element that you have made clear is that the game will not ship with online mode, with a focus on the single player mode. Why was that stand taken, given the scope for enhanced exposure on an online mode option?

Again, this was a really hard decision as we had spent some time  prototyping a fully featured multiplayer component. We made the decision to cut it after E3 2013, when Metro had such a great response from the press and fans for its single player campaign. The choice was – continue with multiplayer, or bring that group back into the main team and focus everything on single player.

Based on the community’s response, I think we made the right decision at the time – but it’s something we would like to return to. We hate to leave work unfinished.

f.

Tell us something on how you developed Artyom ‘s character. Compared to the person unsure of his path in 2033, he seems like a more confident explorer and fighter, is there a greater role in the pipeline for him?

To put it simply, in Metro 2033 Artyom was a ‘boy’ (okay, we was in his early  20’s but he was young, naïve and lived a sheltered life). Metro 2033 was the story of his journey to manhood, his loss of innocence. In Metro: Last Light, Artyom is a man -  jaded, burnt out, and guilty of genocide against the Dark Ones. His story is now one of salvation, redemption… Can he return to innocence? Through the player’s actions, and the way you play as Artyom, that question is answered.

g.

Despite being a linear shooter, Metro: Last Light is a systemic game. We specifically loved the concept of a button mapped specifically for the sole purpose of wiping your face….

And that is just one example! Yes, we wanted to give the player a huge range of controls and actions, far more than you might expect in a straight ‘shooter’ – although as we have said, we think Metro is more than just a shooter! The ability to wipe your mask is, I think, symbolic of our approach to design. It may seem like a small detail, but the cumulative effect of these details is what makes Metro such an immersive whole.

h.

The storyline, in contrast to the environs is very imaginative, with its blend of Nazis, the Communists and the Order. What were the factors you considered while putting together this story structure?

The storyline, we really have Dmitry Glukhovsky to thank for. The balance of power between the warring extremist factions is a theme that runs through both Metro 2033 and Metro: Last Light – it’s Dmitry’s pessimistic (but arguably realistic) metaphor for the nature of mankind, as well as a thinly veiled parable for the contemporary  social and political landscape. That injection of social realism into the storytelling gives us a more believable world than standard post-apocalyptic mythology.

i.

Dmitry Glukhovsky was working deeply with you on the game, what unique element do you think he brought to the game, in terms of characterization, gameplay aspects etc?

Dmitry contributed the key plotline and narrative themes, character studies and character development and the vast majority of actual dialogue, including all the side conversations that do so much to flesh out the game world. Actual scenario and gameplay design was handled by the studio. Having a master story teller like Dmitry involved really helped us elevate the narrative elements of the game, allowing 4A to focus on gameplay.

j.

What is the future of the console, how do you see social networks playing a part of the gaming sphere? Do you think it’s possible to have the same impact as purely console based games? Say, for the ipad or for Android?

All game-playing devices – PC, console, tablet and mobile – serve the needs of the gaming audience. Some game types are (currently) a naturally better fit for high power, high fidelity consoles and PC.

Sure, we will see social and connected features continue to transform console games as they have mobile, but as long as there is an audience for the kind of epic, long-form single player adventure that the console experience can deliver, you will continue to see games like Metro: Last Light being made!

k.

How far are we from putting a player bang in the middle of the action, as a virtual cast member

Doesn’t that already happen? Look at the MMO space, with games like EVE…

l.

Who is your gaming idol, someone whom you have idolized. Tell us of that one game that you would have loved to work on.

We are not so interested in what others are doing – we can only make the games we want to play!

m.

Android or Apple, who is the winner? Or even Windows?

Who the ‘winner’ is is not important here. What are the games? That’s all that matters.

Apple has released the second beta version of iOS 7, this time with support for the iPad.

When Apple released the iOS 7 beta earlier this month, it said the iPad version would be made available “soon”.

Some developers had already hacked it onto iPad devices, but support wasn’t official.

In addition to being available for the iPad, the second version of the beta brings back support for the voice memo app. Apple had left out support for it in the first version of the iOS 7 beta.

It also features many of the new Siri versions that were previewed at the Worldwide Developers Conference two weeks ago.

Registered developers can update their iOS 7 beta through their iPhones if they have the first version installed.

Those developers wanting to install the iOS 7 beta on their iPad will need to do so through iTunes.
